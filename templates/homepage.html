Suspicious Ship-to-Ship transfers
The Problem:

60% of Russia's GDP is directly tied to natural resources. With the EU declaring sanctions on Russian oil, it attempts to hinder Russia's financing of the invasion of Ukraine. However, ships have been finding ways to avoid the sanctions on the high seas. Tankers coming from Russia, carrying sanctioned Russian oil, meet up with another tanker from a different origin. They then transfer the load of the Russian tanker to the non-Russian one. Since the true origin of natural resources like oil and gas is difficult to track, the other ship can sell Russian oil freely in the EU market. This directly funds the Russian invasion of Ukraine.


Learn more here


The Solution:

We built the SOS (Suspicious Oil Shipments) tracker to facilitate accountability even in the middle of the ocean. Our model scrapes AIS data from 27.500 ships capable of carrying oil or gas, and tracks the last port of origin for them. If this origin is on Russian territory, the ships interactions with other vessels is monitored. If the Russian tanker spends extended periods of time in close proximity to another vessel, it is flagged on our website.

If you close this pop-up you can explore our website monitoring suspicous activity between tankers.
